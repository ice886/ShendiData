Bulk element analyses reveal that the rare earth element (REE) distribution pattern exhibits enrichment in light REEs, depletion in heavy REEs and are enriched in high field strength elements (HFSEs) and depleted in large ion lithophile elements (LILEs). The δEu values indicate negative Eu anomalies. The LA–ICP–MS zircon U–Pb ages are 109 ± 1.8 Ma, 115 ± 1.6 Ma and 126 ± 1 Ma, respectively, which are consistent with the range of gold mineralization in Jiaodong Peninsula (JEP). Comparison with the widespread mafic-intermediate-felsic dikes and the regional tectonic evolution of Tan-lu fault, it is concluded that the episodic developments of lithospheric thinning are in accordance with the extensive gold mineralization in the JEP during the Early Cretaceous. The acidic volcanic rocks documented that the JLB evolved from prototype into a volcanic basin, meanwhile, large-scale gold mineralization formed along the secondary branches of the Tan-lu fault in the Early Cretaceous, due to the combined effects of changes in the subduction angle of the Paleo-Pacific Plate, trench migration and changes in the subduction direction. 1. Introduction As one of an ancient craton, the North China Craton (NCC) existed as an intact body until the Mesozoic Era. During the late Mesozoic, the NCC experienced lithospheric thinning and tectonic regime transformation events that were caused by subduction of the Paleo-Pacific plate (PPP), which resulted in the deformation and destruction of the NCC (Zhai and Santosh, 2013; Zhu et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2024). Scholars in earth sci­ ences believe that the subduction and retreat of the western Pacific plate contributed external dynamic stresses that caused deep lithospheric mantle replacement in the eastern part of the NCC and the coupling of shallow basins and mountains. With the retreat of the subduction plate, asthenospheric materials upwelled and migrated upward along the Tan- lu fault zone. The eastern part of the NCC experienced intense destruc­ tion, and large deposits of gold and other metals occur in this area, thus it is a key area for studying the Mesozoic metallogeny and lithospheric evolution of the NCC (Deng et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2020; Zheng and Dai, 2018; B. Zhang et al., 2020a; Yang et al., 2021). The Jiaodong Peninsula (JEP) formed in reactivated Paleoproter­ ozoic terranes ~1.8 b.y. after the occurrence of high-grade meta­ morphism of the Precambrian basement rocks, and the Precambrian geologic terrane that was exposed on the JEP was deformed as a result of the Triassic (240–225 Ma) collision of the North China and South China blocks (B. Zhang et al., 2020a). Early Cretaceous volcanism in the JEP occurred mainly at the Qingshan Stage, and the temporal and spatial distributions of volcanic rocks in the Qingshan Group have been widely studied (Ling et al., 2009; Kuang et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2018; He et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2018). However, the nature of the source region of the volcanic rocks and its implications for the evolution of the Jiaolai Basin (JLB) during the Early Cretaceous remain uncertain. For example, various scholars believe that the volcanic rocks in the Qingshan Group originated from the lower crust and are bimodal volcanic rocks that are related to the subduction of the Yangtze Plate (Yan and Chen, 2007; Fu et al., 2014). More emphasis has been placed on the geochemistry of mafic–intermediate–felsic dikes and their source region analyses, but constraints on the comprehensive regional tectonic evolution and on the coupling of the evolution process of the JLB with large-scale Au mineralization during the Early Cretaceous have not been imposed (Zhou et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2024). Recently, a series of acidic volcanic rocks in the Qingshan Group were found on the western margin of the JLB along the Yishu fault zone (Xu, 2019; Kong et al., 2022). In this study, samples of the newly discovered acidic volcanic rocks in the Qingshan Group on the western margin of the JLB were collected and analyzed; the geochemical char­ acteristics, including major and trace element contents and isotopic chronology, were comprehensively summarized; and the magmatic evolution of the Early Cretaceous volcanic rocks in the JLB was inves­ tigated to determine the properties of the magma source region and the formation process of the Early Cretaceous JLB. This information is highly important for revealing the tectonic evolution of the JLB during the Early Cretaceous and the large-scale gold mineralization in the JEP. 2. Regional geological background The NCC, which is a stable block with a long geological history, provides crustal evolution information covering a period longer than 3.8 billion years (Xu et al., 2009; Zhu et al., 2018). Late Mesozoic large-scale lithospheric thinning of the NCC occurred mainly distributed in the Early Cretaceous (130–110 Ma) (Li et al., 2012; R.X. Zhu et al., 2012b; Zheng et al., 2018). Early Cretaceous volcanic activity was closely related to the evolution of the Tan-lu fault (Zhu et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2020). The Shandong section of the Tan-lu fault is also referred to as the Yishu fault. The fault runs through the middle of Shandong Province and consists of a series of NNE-trending faults, of which includes four main faults. In addition to the main fault, a series of branch faults are present on the western and eastern sides, among which the branch faults in the JEP on the eastern side of the major fault are distributed mainly in the NE direction. The entire Yishu fault can be considered a fault system or rift system (Fig. 1b). A series of NE-trending tensile normal faults and pull-apart basins formed and large-scale magmatic activity occurred in the JEP in direct response to craton thinning in the shallow crust (Kuang et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2018; Deng et al., 2018). The JLB is bounded by the Yishu fault zone to the west, the Wulian–Qingdao fault to the south and the overlapping Jiaobei uplift and Sulu orogenic belt to the north (Fig. 1a). The basement strata in the Jiaobei uplift and JLB include mainly the Neoarchean Jiaodong Group which is dominated by tonalite–­ trondhjemite–granodiorite (TTG) gneisses (Archean basement in Fig. 1a) Paleoproterozoic Fenzishan Groups which are dominated by amphibolite–grade metasedimentary rocks; and the Meso–Neoproter­ ozoic Penglai Group of metasedimentary rocks (Deng et al., 2018). Jiaolai Basin The JLB, which is located at the intersection of a series of large–scale structures, is a Mesozoic continental volcano–sedimentary basin in eastern China, with a length of approximately 500 km from northeast to southwest and a width of approximately 100 km from east to west. The basin consists of a series of grabens that trend E–W to WNW–ESE and is filled with Cretaceous–Cenozoic fluvial lacustrine sedimentary rocks and abundant Mesozoic volcanic rocks (Meng et al., 2022). The forma­ tion and evolution of the basin were influenced by Early Cretaceous rifting and Late Cretaceous pull-apart basin formation, as well as the dextral pull-apart effects of the Tan-lu fault and the Muping–Jimo fault (G. Zhu et al., 2012a; Ji et al., 2022). The inland facies of the basin volcanic–sedimentary rock units are distributed along the depression north of the Wulian–Qingdao fault. Regionally, important branch faults include the Zhaoping fault (also referred to as Pingdu fault), Taocun fault, and Qixia fault on the northern side and the Baichihe fault and Wulian–Qingdao fault on the southern side of the JLB (Fig. 1b). The basin basement composes a Precambrian metamorphic rock se­ ries, which includes mainly the Archean Jiaodong Group, and the Pro­ terozoic Fenzishan Group and Penglai Group. The strata from the Early Cretaceous to the Paleocene in this area are undeformed and continuous, and the Cretaceous strata are most widely exposed. The Fenzishan Group (Fig. 1a) is composed of mica schist, biotite granulite, amphibo­ lite, gneiss and marble (He et al., 2020). Yanshanian (180–90 Ma) magmatic rocks are widespread in and around the JLB, and these Mesozoic magmatic rocks are mainly diorites and granitoids. Plutonic intrusions, epigenetic intrusions, ultraepigenetic intrusions and extru­ sive rock, including mainly rhyolitic volcanic lava and pyroclastic rock, are observed (Cao et al., 2018). The tectonic evolution of the JLB in the Cretaceous can be divided into five stages, and the Qingshan Group formed mainly in an E–W tectonic extensional setting at the late stage of the Early Cretaceous (Ma et al., 2022). The volcanic rocks of the Qingshan Group are the most representative Mesozoic volcanic rocks in the JEP. 2.2. Geological background of the Qingshan Group Magmatism occurred intensely during the Early Cretaceous (also referred to as the Qingshan period) in the eastern part of the NCC, which resulted in the formation of the volcanic rocks in the Qingshan Group (Cao et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019). Regionally, magmatic rocks are widely distributed in the Yishu fault zone, Luxi and JEP, and they were highly important for constraining magmatic activity during the late Early Cretaceous and the tectonic background in this area. Geological background The study area occurs on the eastern side of the Yishu fault at the western margin of the JLB (Fig. 2a). The recently discovered outcropped acidic rocks is located on the northern side of the Xiashan Reservoir and comprises mainly of a set of intermediate–acidic pyroclastic rocks. The eastern and western sides of the study area are controlled by two NE–SW-trending faults, whereas the southern and northern sides are controlled by an E–W-trending fault (Fig. 3). The outcropping strata in the study area are composed mainly of the Paleoproterozoic Fenzishan Group, the Early Cretaceous Laiyang Group, the Qingshan Group and Quaternary strata. In a previous study, the rhyolite from the Gaomi area which is 10 km east of the study area, yielded an average age of 113.7 ± 0.51 Ma, and the ages of individual zircons ranged mainly from 110 to 119 Ma. The ages of individual zircons from the rhyolitic tuff range from 109 to 130 Ma, and the average age is 119.57 ± 0.44 Ma (Kong et al., 2022). The Cretaceous continental “red layer” of rhyolitic ignimbrite from Wanjia to Lancun to the eastern part of the study area formed during a period of volcanic activity at 118.32 ± 0.73 Ma, which is approximately equiva­ lent to the age of the upper Shiqianzhuang Formation in the region. The boreholes of Jiaoshen 2 and Jiaoshen 3 are relatively complete; the lower parts of these boreholes intersect the Bamu di Formation of the Cretaceous Qingshan Group, and the upper parts cross the Quaternary unconformity (Xu, 2019). Sample collection The samples that were examined in this study were collected from the northern Xiashan Reservoir at the western margin of the JLB (Figs. 1b and 3). Bulk samples with fresh surfaces, hard and dense tex­ tures, and low degrees of alteration were selected. Field outcrop and microscopy observations revealed that the sampled rocks were mainly acidic volcanic rocks. In accordance with the results of petrographic identification, representative samples were selected for bulk element analyses. The geochronological samples that were collected in the field weighed approximately 5 kg and were crushed to 80–120 mesh. After the powdered samples were washed with water, magnetic minerals such as magnetite were removed with magnets, and zircon grains were manually picked under a binocular microscope. According to the rock identification analyses, the rocks are mainly subsurface rhyolite, brec­ ciated ignimbrite and rhyolitic tuff. The microscopic characteristics of the representative samples are presented below. 3.2. Petrological characteristics 3.2.1. Subrhyolite The subrhyolite rocks were light gray, purplish gray, and grayish white in color (Fig. 4a). The rock fabrics were porphyritic and blocky. These rocks were composed mainly of porphyritic material and matrix (Fig. 4d). The phenocrysts were potassium feldspar (5 %) and quartz (1 % ~ 5 %), and the proportion of biotite was low. The potassium feldspar, had a semi-idiomorphic plate shape and ranged in size from 0.2 to 0.5 mm, with small amounts ranging in size from 0.5 to 1 mm and feldspar featured a scattered distribution. The quartz was semi-idiomorphic granular and ranged in size from 0.2 to 0.5 mm, with small amounts of particles that ranged from 0.5 to 1 mm. The biotite was flake shaped and 0.2–0.5 mm in diameter and was distributed in a scattered manner, and it was present in multiple colors (Fig. 4g). The matrix comprised potassium feldspar (ca. 50 %), plagioclase (ca. 25 %), and quartz (ca.25 %). Together, the quartz and feldspar form a microcrystalline texture with a disordered distribution. 3.2.2. Brecciated rhyolite The brecciated rhyolite rock exhibited a porphyritic texture and was composed mainly of phenocrysts (ca. 10 %), matrix (ca. 80 %) and a small amount of rock debris (Fig. 4b). The color was mainly light flesh red and purplish gray. The phenocrysts were quartz pyroclasts and plagioclase pyroclasts, which were generally angular in shape, their sizes were generally 2–3 mm, and they were scattered and oriented (Fig. 4e). The matrix had a cryptocrystalline texture and consisted of crystal pyroclasts (30 % ~ 35 %), vitric pyroclast (50 % ~ 55 %) and rock debris (5 % ~ 10 %) and the structure was characterized by a breccia-welded tuff texture and pseudorhyolite structure (Fig. 4h). The crystal pyroclasts comprised plagioclase, quartz and potassium feldspar, and were angularly distributed, scattered and slightly oriented. The vitric pyroclasts were plastic (striated and transcrystalline). The rock debris was plastic and was mainly rhyolite, lenticular or striate; the size was generally 1.5–2.5 mm, with a small portion of size 0.4–1 mm, and the debris was scattered and oriented. Analytical methods Analyses of the main and trace element contents in the volcanic rocks were performed at the experimental test center of the fourth Geological Group of the Shandong Provincial Bureau of Geology and Mineral Exploration and Development. Geochemical analysis of major and trace elements was performed after the sample fragments were crushed to 200 mesh. Major elements were analyzed using the alkali fusion glass X-ray fluorescence (XRF) method. Samples of trace elements and rare earth elements (REEs) were prepared and analyzed by the acid dissolution method, and then tested by an Agilent 7500 ICP–MS instrument. The analysis accuracy was greater than 5 %, whereas that for the trace ele­ ments was better than 10 %. Isotopic dating sample crushing, zircon sorting, cath­ odoluminescence (CL) imaging and target fabrication were completed by the Test Center Laboratory of the Shandong Bureau of the China Metallurgical Geology Administration. The zircon sorting process was completed via conventional single mineral sorting technology. The tar­ gets were cured with epoxy resin and polished until the zircon interiors were exposed. Finally, the internal structure of the zircon was analyzed with a TESCAN MIRA3 field emission scanning electron microscope, and CL micrographs were obtained. Laser ablation inductively coupled plasma–mass spectrometry (LA–ICP–MS) was performed with a Geo­ LasPro193 nm ArF excimer system, which was produced by the Coherent Company in the United States, and the ICP–MS model was an iCAPQ produced by Thermo Fisher Company. The diameter of the ablation spot was 30 μm. The zircon standard that was used as the external standard for calibration was Pleˇsovice, and the external stan­ dard for trace elements was NIST SRM 610, where Zr was the internal standard element. The element contents and isotope ratios of the sam­ ples were calculated using the ICPMSDATACAL data processing pro­ gram. The ISOPLOT 4.0 program was used to construct an age distribution frequency plot and U–Pb age concordia plot and to perform weighted average age calculations. 4. Analysis results 4.1. Zircon U–Pb chronology The CL images revealed that the zircon crystal shape was intact, and that the zircon grains were mostly subangular to subcircular in shape. The major axis of the zircon grains generally ranged from 75 to 200 μm, and the length-to-width ratio ranged from 1 to 2.7. The CL images indicate that the internal oscillatory zone in the zircon grains was clear (Fig. 5a). The oscillatory zone was narrow, and was characterized by magmatic zircon. The zircon U–Pb ages are relatively concentrated in the concordia plot (Fig. 5b, c and d), and the Th/U values were greater than 0.4 for D5003 and D5006, and range from 0.34 to 1.17 for D5001, respectively (Supplementary Table 1). The weighted average ages were 109 ± 1.8 Ma, 115 ± 1.6 Ma and 126 ± 1 Ma, respectively, indicating that the sampled volcanic rocks in the Xiashan area formed at the late stage of the Early Cretaceous. 4.2. Geochemical characteristics of major trace elements The petrochemical characteristics of the Cretaceous volcanic rocks in the Xiashan area are listed in Supplementary Table 2. The contents of SiO2 in the rocks ranged from 71.01 % to 78.06 %, with an average of 74.75 %. The Al2O3 content varies from 11.78 % to 15.84 %, with an average of 13.45 %. In the diagram of the aluminum saturation index (ASI) of the volcanic rocks (Fig. 6a), the volcanic samples are all plotted in the peraluminous region, which indicates that the volcanic rocks were mainly peraluminous volcanic rocks, with an average ASI of 1.82 %. The Rittman index(σ) ranged from 0.98 to 2.54. The K2O content of the rocks was high and ranged from 4.70 % ~ 6.39 %, the Na2O content ranged from 0.54 % ~ 2.88 %, and the K2O/Na2O value was 1.92–8.70, which indicates that the rocks were rich in potassium and alkalis. Thus, these rocks belonged to the potassium-rich calc-alkaline (HKCA)-shoshonite series (Fig. 6b). The CIA value varied from 53.02 to 70.61, with an average of 57.76; generally, the higher the CIA value is, the more of K, Na and Ca elements are leached from the mother rock (Nesbitt and Young, 1982; Ohta and Arai, 2007), and our datasets fall into the “weak” weathering category (Fedo et al., 1995; CIA = 50–60, weak; CIA = 60–80, intermediate; and CIA = 80–100, strong). The oxidation coeffi­ cient (Fe2O3/(Fe2O3 + FeO)) values of the samples are generally high. In the total alkali–silica (TAS) diagram (Fig. 7), the sample points are plotted in the rhyolite region. The figure reveals that the volcanic rocks in the study area are subalkaline. In general, the volcanic rocks in the Xiashan area are characterized by calc-alkaline high‑potassium rhyolite. The results of the analysis of rare earth elements (REEs) are listed in Supplementary Table 2. The total amounts of REEs in the rocks ranged from 85.36 × 10−6 to 218.75 × 10−6, the contents of light REEs (LREEs) ranged from 66.69 × 10−6 to 206.18 × 10−6, and the contents of heavy REEs (HREEs) ranged from 10.48 × 10−6 to 51.23 × 10−6. The ratios of LREEs and HREEs ranged from 1.30 to 14.77, the normalized (La/Yb) values ranged from 3.50 to 52.52, and the (La/Sm)N values ranged from 3.18 to 8.60; all these values were greater than 1. Furthermore, the chondrite-normalized REE pattern shows a relative LREE enrichment (Fig. 8a), which indicates relative enrichment in light REEs (LREEs) and relative depletion in heavy REEs (HREEs). The degree of fractionation of the LREEs was greater than that of the HREEs. The fractionation of the LREEs and HREEs was obvious, and the δEu value varied from 0.08 to 1.09 (and was mostly less than 0.5), with an obvious negative anomaly. Previous studies have concluded that tetrad effects can be divided into four sections according to the variation of in the sample/chondrite curves for highly evolved granitoids (Irber, 1999), as shown in Fig. 8a. In this study, the Y/Ho, Sr/Eu, Zr/Hf and δEu values were generally high, and could be explained by the interactions between the chlorine-dominated fluids and residual melts in the open system of highly evolved acidic magmas (Peretyazhko and Savina, 2010). The results of the analysis of the trace element contents in the vol­ canic rocks in the Xiashan area are listed in Supplementary Table 2. A spidergram of the trace elements (Fig. 8b) shows that various large-ion lithophile elements (LILEs) (such as K) and HFSEs (such as Th) exhibit positive anomalies and are relatively enriched, whereas other LILEs (such as Ba and Sr) and high-field strength elements (HFSEs) (such as Nb and Ti) exhibited negative anomalies and were relatively depleted. Especially, Pb showed strong positive anomalies with low Ni contents, which indicated crustal involvement and correlation with plate subduction. 5. Discussion and results 5.1. Zhang et al., 2020b; Koua et al., 2022; H.Y. Wang et al., 2024a), which is consistent with the extensive gold mineralization in the JEP and the destruction of the NCC in the eastern China, with a peak age of ca. 120 Ma (Fig. 9). 5.1.2. Source region characteristics The lithospheric mantle in the northern part of Luxi is characterized mainly by enriched mantle type I (EMI), which provides records of the signature of the lower crust of the NCC. The convecting mantle mixed with the foundering lithospheric peridotite, which had previously experienced subduction-related melt metasomatism during the collision of the Yangtze and NCC blocks (Ling et al., 2009). The ca. 0.7 Ga intermediate–basic volcanic rocks indicate that these rocks were influ­ enced not only by ancient crustal materials in the NCC but also by deeply subducted materials in the Yangtze continental crust (Liu et al., 2018; Ma et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024). Mafic dykes from the Jiaobei uplift and Sulu orogenic belt exhibit arc-like trace element signatures, which demonstrates that these signa­ tures originated from metasomatically enriched mantle sources (Deng et al., 2017; H.Y. Wang et al., 2024a). The Early Cretaceous potassic volcanic rocks in the Sulu orogenic belt and the intermediate–basic volcanic rocks and basic dikes in the JEP are enriched in LILEs and LREEs and depleted in HFSEs and have high 87Sr/86Sr values and low Nd (t) and negative Hf(t) isotope characteristics. These features are also interpreted as being derived from the metasomatism-enriched litho­ spheric mantle (Cao et al., 2020; L. Wang et al., 2024b). Therefore, the rhyolite in the JEP was formed by deep melting in the background of regional extension and high thermal anomalies, and a large amount of plagioclase fractional crystallization may have occurred during magma ascent, which is in accordance to that of the extensive gold minerali­ zation in the JEP. 5.2. Tectonic evolution of the JLB in the Early Cretaceous 5.2.1. Modification of PPP subduction The subduction and recirculation of the PPP during the Mesozoic were key drives of the formation and evolution of eastern China and led to lateral orogenic accretion of the crust. Furthermore, these processes profoundly affect the deep material recycling process, they directly led to deep lithospheric mantle replacement and caused shallow basin and mountain coupling processes in the eastern part of the NCC (Zheng and Dai, 2018; Ma et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2019; Zhu and Xu, 2019; Meng et al., 2022). The subducted lithospheric mantle of the PPP extended deep beneath the interior of the NCC, and was associated with tectono- magmatic activity (Zheng et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2024). The influence of the PPP on the eastern part of the NCC is reflected in the following main aspects: 1) During the Early Cretaceous, the PPP subduction style changed from low-angle (flat) to high-angle (Zhu and Xu, 2019; Yang et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024; Ni et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). From 130 to 120 Ma, the PPP may have experienced high-angle subduction, thus reaching a maximum rate of rollback and retreat, and the slab was eventually retained in the mantle transition zone (Zhu and Xu, 2019), which caused intense deformation in the eastern part of the NCC and resulted in the formation of massive amounts of magma and gold and other metal deposits (Zhu et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2021); 2) The PPP subduction retreated (Zhang et al., 2014; G. Zhu et al., 2012a; Zhang et al., 2016). The lithospheric extension and basin formation in eastern China were closely related to the subduction angle and retreat of the PPP (Liu et al., 2024). The PPP began to retreat ca. 145 Ma, which may have resulted in the formation of the large Cretaceous East Asian mantle wedge above the mantle transition zone (Xu et al., 2009; Zhu et al., 2018). This may be explained by the sub­ duction and closure of the Okhotsk Ocean in the north and the New Tethys Ocean in the south, which pushed the mantle asthenosphere under the NCC to the east and forced the PPP to subduct and retreat to the east. This process differs from the flat subduction and trench attraction that were caused by the high-angle subduction that moved the mantle asthenosphere eastward (Kusky et al., 2014). 3) The subduction direction changed (Liu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Ni et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024), which may have been the main factor that led to changes in the tectonic stress direction in eastern China. The eastern part of the NCC experienced intense lithospheric extension from ca. 135 Ma to 105 Ma (Supplementary Table 3; Fig. 10), for example, the metamorphic core complex in the JEP experienced rapid exhumation from 135 to 123 Ma (Ni et al., 2016). At ca. 145 Ma, the Izanagi plate shifted from low-speed NW-trending movement to high-speed north- trending movement, which changed the stress state in North China and generated a large amount of extensional tectono-magmatic activity (Zheng and Dai, 2018). The retreat of the subduction plate led to a highly extensional setting, and the change in the subduction direction at 122 Ma was the main factor that influenced large-scale magmatic ac­ tivity and gold mineralization in the JEP (Ni et al., 2024). The large-scale tectono-magmatism in eastern China during the Early Cretaceous may have been the result of the combined effect of changes in the PPP subduction angle, trench retreat and subduction direction. From 130 to 110 Ma, the lithospheric mantle in eastern China was dominated by the subduction of the PPP. The forward-retreat variation in the subduction plate, especially the change in the subduction direc­ tion ca. 120 Ma, may have been the main factor that controlled basin formation and large-scale gold mineralization in eastern China. There­ fore, both the gold deposits and volcanic rocks in the JEP were the result of episodic processes of PPP subduction modification during the Early Cretaceous. 5.2.2. Implication of Tan-lu fault activity The Tan-lu fault zone served as a favorable channel for magma ascent during the lithospheric thinning process in eastern China (G. Zhu et al., 2012a; Zhu et al., 2018). A comparative study of mantle enclaves along the fault zone revealed that mantle metasomatism in the fault zone is strong, which indicates that the fault zone is characterized by the up­ welling of juvenile asthenospheric material and lithospheric mantle modification and replacement. Thus, this fault zone provides important insights into the coupled relationship between tectonic evolution and oceanic subduction (Zheng and Dai, 2018; Zhu et al., 2018). The Tan-lu fault zone experienced strong left-lateral strike-slip movement during the Early Cretaceous, with an offset distance greater than 500 km (G. Zhu et al., 2012a; Zhu et al., 2018). The extensional activity of the Tan-lu fault zone during the Early Cretaceous can be divided into the following two stages: an early stage of strong magma­ tism (135–115 Ma) and a late stage of weakened magmatism (115–100 Ma) (Zhou et al., 2015). These were major stages in the formation and evolution of volcanic rocks in the JLB and correspond to scenarios B and C of the Yanshanian tectonic belt in the northern NCC (Zhu et al., 2018). The early Cretaceous extension in the Tan-lu fault zone can be further divided into the following two stages: 1) The early stage (135–120 Ma in the Laiyang Stage) was controlled by NW–SE extension, and normal faults and subsidence developed mainly along the northern detachment boundary of the Sulu orogenic belt, with little volcanism in the JLB. 2) The late stage of E–W extension (120–100 Ma in the Qingshan period) was controlled by the narrow rift mode, and the Tan-lu fault zone collapsed, and transformed into a zone of highly active normal faults with rapid subsidence and intense volcanism. During this period, the JLB evolved into a typical intracontinental volcanic graben basin (Zheng and Dai, 2018). Extensive volcanism and extensive gold deposits developed at ca. 120 Ma along the secondary faults of the Tan-lu fault zone (B. Zhang et al., 2020a; Zhu et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2020; Ji et al., 2022; Ni et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024), such as the Zhaoping fault, Qixia fault, Taocun fault and Muping-Jimo fault (Fig. 1b), and these faults were originally formed during the Jurassic and then reactivated during the Early Cretaceous (Deng et al., 2017). The enriched subcontinental lithospheric mantle contributed continuously to the generation of syntectonic magmas in the JEP starting at 135 Ma, but the number of magmas that were derived from juvenile and depleted subcontinental lithospheric mantle in the JEP sharply increased starting at ca. 122 Ma (Liu et al., 2020). Studies on intermediate–mafic dyke swarms suggest that the JEP lithosphere experienced two stages of Early Cretaceous tectonic evolu­ tion: an early stage (135–122 Ma), which was characterized by large- scale tectonic extension of the crust and limited magmatic activity in L. The rapid transition of magma sources may suggest that the lithosphere detached and was removed locally beneath the JEP at ca. 121 Ma (Liu et al., 2020), which is in accordance with the peak of the extensive gold mineralization. Multiple provenance analyses in the JLB have revealed a history of northward expansion of rift events, which is in accordance with the change in the Izanagi subduction direction that may have occurred at 121 Ma (Zhu et al., 2018; Ni et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). 5.2.3. Tectonic evolution model of the JLB According to the above discussion, the early Cretaceous evolution of the JLB can be divided into the following two stages. 1) From 135 to 120 Ma, as the lithosphere of the eastern part of the NCC further shrunk, flexed, thickened and sank, the tectonic regime switched rapidly from WNW–ESE compression to NW–SE extension (Fig. 11a), which resulted in the formation of numerous metamorphic core complexes, such as the Queshan metamorphic core complex, Wulian metamorphic core complex and Guojialing granite in the Jiaobei uplift (133–126 Ma) (Ma et al., 2014). Carbonated magmatic activity occurred in the central and eastern NCC at 132–124 Ma; however, these activities were still spatially constrained to the early orogenic belt or a deep large fault zone (Li and Hou, 2018). The large-scale extensional tectonics in the JEP were accompanied by low-angle (normal) detach­ ment faults and the Wulian, Queshan and Linglong metamorphic core complexes (Ni et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2020; Ji et al., 2022). Rapid detachment of the Wulian fault occurred between 128 and 123 Ma, which is consistent with the peak period of lithospheric thinning of the NCC in the late Mesozoic (Ni et al., 2016). A wide range of basic dikes formed in the Jiaobei uplift (Liu et al., 2018; H.Y. Wang et al., 2024a), and the subduction direction of the PPP under high-angle subduction changed to the NW, which produced WNW–ESE extension, thus inducing widespread intracontinental extension in the eastern part of the NCC and large-scale thinning of the overlying lithosphere. From 135 to 120 Ma, the subducted PPP continued to roll back eastward, and the lithosphere of the NCC thinned in response to regional extension and delamination. Asthenospheric mantle upwelling led to partial melting of the lower crust, which generated the basic veins and parental magma of rhyolite and gold mineralization in the JEP. 2) During the late Early Cretaceous (120–110 Ma), with the sub­ duction of the PPP beneath the Eurasian plate, a WNW–ESE extensional regime formed, which drove the early formation of the JLB and influ­ enced the late orogenic process of the Sulu orogenic belt. Moreover, the compressive stress of the Tan-lu fault decreased, and a series of exten­ sional normal faults and graben basins developed in the upper crust, thereby resulting in rapid subsidence of the Tan-lu fault zone, which was accompanied by strong volcanism (Fig. 11b). The JLB also began to rapidly collapse and pull apart, and with the eruption of volcanic rocks in a large area of the Qingshan Group, it evolved into a volcanic basin (He et al., 2020). During this period, extensional rift basins developed along the existing weak zones and controlled the formation and distri­ bution of the volcanic rocks in the Qingshan Group in the JEP. The oblique subduction of the PPP (Izanagi plate) triggered left-lateral strike-slip movement of the Tan-lu fault and the secondary ENE-NE- trending faults (G. Zhu et al., 2012a), which formed channels and de­ posit positions for the extensive gold-bearing magmatic–hydrothermal mineralization. 6. Conclusions 1) The LA–ICP–MS zircon U–Pb ages of the volcanic rocks in the Xiashan area to the east of the Tan-lu fault zone are 109 ± 1.8 Ma, 115 ± 1.6 Ma and 126 ± 1 Ma, respectively; these rocks formed during the late Early Cretaceous and are assigned to the Shiqianzhuang Formation in the Qingshan Group at the western margin of the JLB. The rocks are highly acidic and rich in potassium and aluminum and belong to the calc-alkaline series. The rocks are enriched in LREEs and have strong negative Eu anomalies. Additionally, these rocks are enriched in HFSEs and depleted in LILEs. 2) On the basis of the geochemical analyses of volcanic rocks and the tectonic characteristics of the regional Tan-lu fault, the tectonic evolu­ tion of the Early Cretaceous JLB can be divided into two episodes: (1) From ca.135 to ca.120 Ma, the lithosphere of the NCC thinned in response to regional extension and delamination, which generated the basic veins and parental magma of rhyolite and initiated the formation of the JLB. (2) From ca.120 to ca.110 Ma, the framework of the JLB essentially formed and evolved into a volcanic basin, and large-scale gold mineralization occurred along the secondary branches of the Tan- lu fault. 3) The tectonic evolution of the JLB during the Early Cretaceous can be attributed to the change in subduction angle from low to high, and the combination of the change in the subduction direction of the PPP and the retreat of the subducting plate may have been the main factor that controlled the formation of large-scale volcanism in the JLB.
